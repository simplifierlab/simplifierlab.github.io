Customer-focused prioritization, and continuous testing and iteration: the keys to shipping fine-grained permissions v.1 in 4 months

Customer problem

Admins of large GitHub organizations had long requested the need for more granular control over who has access to and what actions those users can take over certain parts of their account.

As a way to simplify access control, GitHub bundles the ability to take actions based on roles, i.e., whether you’re a member or admin. But, in some cases, those role-based permissions were either too lax or too restrictive.

Admins imagined a solution where they’re able to allow or disallow a subset of actions per user for each feature at their discretion.

Challenges

Role-based permissions are foundational to how GitHub works. Changing the permission model not only has widespread impact on the core platform, but also GitHub Enterprise (cloud and on-prem), the API, and thousands of third-party apps in its ecosystem.

And offering fine-grained discretionary control for every feature, every action within a feature, for every user in their organization would create an unwieldy n! combinatorial possibilities that would be impossible for admins to manage â€” especially given the scale of their organizations. Not to mention that this would also create tremendous backend and UI complexity for us.

Product strategy

We understood that at the root of the customer problem was security, i.e., enabling users in an organization to have just enough permissions to do what they’re tasked with — no more and no less. Closing the biggest security gaps defined our North Star rather than prioritizing by contract value or number of requests.
