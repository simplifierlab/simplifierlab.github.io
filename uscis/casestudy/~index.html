Case study:USCIS

The United States Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS) approached 18F to help the agency apply human-centered methods in re-imagining immigration services. But, with mounting pain points and unending opportunities for improvement, turns out, the trickiest challenge was aligning on priorities and pivoting on product strategy.

Oct 2015

The brief: Enable online applications for US citizenship

The vision

Since the process to become a US citizen was largely paper-based and fragmented, USCIS believed that a unified, web application would help reduce errors and cut processing times by reconciling disparate, analog-based records while also offering applicants timely feedback on their application status.

My role

On my first day at 18F, I was dispatched to join the USCIS engagement. For the past year, the 18F team had already been at work on the digital transformation of the Application for Citizenship (N-400). I took over as the Engagement Lead for the PM who was about to go on maternity leave.

The team

The 18F design team included 2 UX designers, 2 content designers, and a visual designer. They reportedly had a tough relationship with the USCIS partners. With 5 months left on the engagement, I was entrusted with ensuring we deliver on expectations and landing the project on good terms. It’s probably worth mentioning that, other than being an immigrant myself, I don't have a background in immigration or any prior experience in government.

Late Oct–Dec 2015

User insights, finally

By the time I joined the team, the application was already in engineering yet had never been validated with users. I urged our USCIS partners to let us conduct user interviews. Structured in the guise of a usability test, we developed a research plan to also help us validate mission-critical assumptions about the primary target users and assess whether the web app would be useful.

The answer varied depending on who I asked which, on its own, indicated a lack of deep understanding of who they're designing for. We eventually settled on a working hypothesis that our primary target users — i.e., early adopters — are highly tech-savvy and are used to conducting highly-sensitive online transactions. Who’s the primary target user?
Recruiting research participants is never easy. Finding naturalization applicants was even more so because we had to walk the fine line of federal protocols — including murky guidance on PRA implications and offering incentives. Along with our USCIS partners, we obtained clearance and completed 33 intercept interviews in situ in Chicago, New York, and San Francisco USCIS field offices over an 8-week span. We selected those locations because they had the best-fitting, demographically representative samples of N400 applicants. Challenges with recruiting participants
33 user sessions / 3 field offices / 8 weeks

Key findings

  • “The forms are easy to fill out but I’d still have my lawyer handle it for me. I’m not going to scan all those documents.”

  • “I don’t have a scanner or computer at home.” Translated from Spanish off the participant’s iPhone

  • “It’s not that much different than the PDF except printing out the PDF made it easier to figure out.”

Among the tech-savvy participants, we found that most were unlikely to use the app and file on their own, opting instead to hire professional services to file on their behalf. Of the remaining participants, lack of access to desktop technology and insufficient guidance and transparency into the journey ahead created barriers that made the existing PDFs the preferable option.

Jan 2016

Design sprint: Guiding a product pivot

Flush with clearer insights about our users, we regrouped with USCIS and invited subject-matter experts to participate in a week-long design sprint. We needed to make the Application for Citizenship more accessible to a population that had rapidly shifted to mobile. And we needed to give them the confidence to file online on their own.

  • The “eligibility tool“ guides users toward their best options and filters ineligible applicants from wasting time and money

  • Using the camera to scan their green card reduces redundant, manual data entry and simplifies data collection

  • In under a minute, users get clear, specific feedback about their eligibility and expected next steps

Mar 2016 — present

Wrap-up: The launch and beyond

USCIS extended their original agreement with 18F and asked us to help them build their own “in-house 18F“ team. They still continue to maintain the online N-400 application form and eligibility tool. And the N-400 served as a model for other online USCIS services that are visited by millions of people each month.
