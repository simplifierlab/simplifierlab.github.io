Case study:USCIS

On the heels of the much-improved relaunch of Healthcare.gov, a number of federal agencies wanted to follow suit and embrace a human-centered approach to designing public services. Accordingly, the United States Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS) approached 18F to help them modernize immigration services. But, with mounting pain points and unending opportunities for improvement, turns out, the trickiest challenge was aligning on priorities and pivoting on product strategy.

Oct 2015

The brief: Enable online applications for US citizenship

The vision

On my first day at 18F, I was immediately deployed to join the USCIS engagement team who, for the past year, had been tasked with the digital transformation of the Application for Citizenship (N-400).

Since the application process was largely paper-based and fragmented, the belief was that a unified, digital online application would help reduce errors and cut processing times by reconciling disparate, analog-based records while also offering applicants timely feedback on their application status.

The team

I took over as the Engagement Lead for the PM who was about to go on maternity leave. The 18F design team included 2 UX designers, 2 content designers, and a visual designer.

The 18F team reportedly had a tense relationship with the USCIS partners. With 5 months left on the engagement, I was entrusted with ensuring we deliver on expectations and land the project on good terms. It’s probably worth mentioning that, aside from being an immigrant myself, I’m no immigration expert nor did I have any prior experience in government.

Late Oct–Dec 2015

User insights, finally

Since the online application was already in development but hadn’t yet been validated with users, I urged our USCIS partners to let us conduct user interviews. Structured in the guise of a usability test, we developed a research plan to also help us validate mission-critical assumptions about the primary target users and whether the web app would be useful.
